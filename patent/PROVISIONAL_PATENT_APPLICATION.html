Chronometric Frequency-Offset Wireless Synchronization System

Inventor: Hunter Bown
Assignee: Shannon Labs, Inc. (Delaware C‑Corp), Division: Driftlock

Abstract

The Bown Driftlock Method introduces chronometric interferometry for wireless synchronization by intentionally maintaining a controlled carrier-frequency offset Δf between nodes. Simultaneous transmission and reception produce a beat signal whose phase evolution encodes both propagation delay τ and residual frequency difference. Closed-form estimators recover τ and Δf over microsecond observation windows without iterative search, while bidirectional measurements separate geometric delay from clock bias. A variance-weighted distributed consensus (optionally seeded by lightweight Kalman filters) propagates pairwise estimates across arbitrary network topologies without a master reference. Hardware-in-the-loop simulations with commodity TCXO/OCXO profiles demonstrate 22.13 ps RMS timing error on dense 64-node networks and 20.09 ps RMS at 512 nodes, confirming scalability with commercial components suitable for 5G/6G coordination, distributed sensing, financial timestamping, quantum networking, and industrial IoT deployments.

Field of the Invention

The invention relates to wireless time and frequency synchronization via chronometric interferometry wherein intentional carrier frequency offsets produce beat signals used for precise delay and frequency‑difference estimation across distributed nodes.

Background

Conventional systems treat frequency offset as an impairment to be eliminated (e.g., IEEE 1588, 3GPP, ITU, IETF, PLL design). GPS/GNSS offers ~10–50 ns timing but fails indoors and is vulnerable to interference. Wired timing (PTP/NTP) degrades in wireless environments. Fiber‑based sub‑nanosecond solutions require specialized infrastructure. Master‑based wireless schemes suffer single points of failure and error accumulation. There remains a need for a masterless, wireless method achieving <10 ns—preferably <2 ns—using commercial oscillators.

Related techniques are distinguishable: FMCW radar uses beats for ranging, not clock synchronization; heterodyne receivers mix to an IF for demodulation rather than treating beat phase as a chronometric observable; and two‑way time transfer (satellite/fiber) exchanges timing signals without intentionally introducing and maintaining an offset Δf within a wireless channel to create a chronometric beat for closed‑form τ/Δf estimation and masterless distributed consensus.

Summary

The method intentionally generates and maintains a small Δf between nodes so that simultaneous transmission and reception yield a beat signal at Δf with a phase that evolves linearly in time and depends on propagation delay. Multi‑carrier signal combs (including missing‑fundamental variants) provide synthetic wavelengths for robust unwrapping, while longer observation windows and low‑phase‑noise oscillators enable an atomic‑mode operating point. By measuring beat phase over microsecond windows and applying a closed‑form estimator, nodes extract τ and Δf without iterative search. A hybrid coherent/aperture processing chain fuses carrier‑phase and envelope estimates to maintain performance across a wide SNR range. Two‑way exchange separates geometric delay from clock bias. A variance‑weighted consensus, optionally preceded by a local Kalman pre‑filter and supported by cyclostationary excitation signatures, achieves network‑wide alignment without a master reference, with spectral step size and optional Chebyshev acceleration for rapid convergence.

Detailed Description

I. Theory of Operation

Nodes i and j employ carriers fi and fj=fi+Δf. Simultaneous transmit/receive produces a beat at Δf with phase φbeat(t) = 2πΔf (t − τ) + (θi − θj) + 2π fi τ. Linear fitting of unwrapped phase yields slope a ≈ 2πΔf and intercept b from which a closed‑form τ is derived using known carrier terms. Here, fi denotes the absolute carrier frequency (Hz) of node i; Δf = fj − fi is the intentional offset maintained as a measurement feature.

II. Two‑Way Handshake

Forward (A→B) and reverse (B→A) measurements produce (τ̂AB, Δf̂AB) and (τ̂BA, Δf̂BA). Bias δt = 0.5(τ̂AB − τ̂BA); geometric delay τgeo = 0.5(τ̂AB + τ̂BA).

Hardware Reciprocity Calibration

Optional calibration modes estimate transmit (TX) and receive (RX) hardware delays per node using loopback fixtures or controlled reference exchanges. A reciprocity bias for link (i,j) is computed as (ΔTX,i − ΔRX,i) − (ΔTX,j − ΔRX,j), and subsequent τ,Δf estimates are compensated by subtracting the derived offset. Calibration parameters integrate with the consensus weights so that residual uncertainty is reflected in the variance model.

III. Estimation and Signal Processing

Pipeline: filter near Δf → sub‑sample (~2×Δf) → angle and unwrap → linear fit φ(t)=at+b → obtain Δf̂ and τ candidates → unwrap τ using coarse hints and/or retunes. Weight multi‑carrier τ estimates proportional to f². Coarse wideband preambles provide unambiguous hints for unwrapping.

Multi‑Carrier Comb Generation

Each node synthesizes a programmable multi‑carrier comb with tones spaced by Δf and, in some embodiments, by integer multiples kΔf to provide synthetic wavelengths for phase unwrapping. The comb is generated by digitally modulating a numerically controlled oscillator bank, allowing rapid retunes between carrier groups. During a measurement epoch the comb may include primary tones f0±mΔf (m∈ℤ) alongside a suppressed fundamental, enabling Chinese Remainder based unwrapping when combined with the baseline beat measurement. The intentional offset Δf is maintained as a synchronization feature rather than corrected as an impairment.

Atomic Mode Operation

For applications demanding sub‑nanosecond accuracy, an atomic mode extends the observation window beyond 100 µs while employing low phase‑noise oscillators (e.g., −120 dBc/Hz at 10 kHz) or disciplined oven‑controlled sources. The longer integration window reduces estimator variance proportionally, and the consensus layer remains unchanged apart from reduced weighting variance.

Missing‑Fundamental Mode

In environments with front‑end filtering near Δf, the comb can be transmitted with the Δf tone digitally suppressed. The receiving node recovers the beat through envelope detection or cyclostationary correlation on the remaining harmonic structure, preserving τ observability while complying with spectral masks. This mode pairs with the aperture processing path described below.

Hybrid Coherent and Aperture Fusion

The coherent branch estimates per‑carrier phases using complex baseband samples from the sub‑sampling ADC, feeding the closed‑form estimator described above. In parallel, a non‑coherent aperture branch computes an envelope‑based time of arrival using matched filtering and fractional delay interpolation. A fusion engine selects the more reliable estimate based on instantaneous signal‑to‑noise ratio or blends the estimates via variance‑weighted averaging. This hybrid approach maintains lock during low SNR or deep fades while retaining coherent precision when conditions allow.

IV. Distributed Consensus

For graph G=(V,E), edge measurements dij with variances σ² update xi=[ΔTi, Δfi] via xi(k+1)=xi(k)+ε Σ Wij(dij−(xi−xj)), with Wij=diag(1/σ²τ,1/σ²Δf). A spectral step size ε≈c/λmax(L), 0<c<1, ensures stability; optional Chebyshev acceleration improves convergence.

Local Kalman Pre‑Filter

Prior to consensus, each node may execute a lightweight two‑state Kalman filter that tracks local clock bias and frequency offset using incoming pairwise measurements as observations. The predictor models oscillator drift and random walk, while the update step uses the latest variance‑weighted residuals. The filtered state seeds the consensus iteration and supplies per‑node covariance estimates that populate the weight matrices Wij, improving robustness under packet loss, asynchronous scheduling, and heterogeneous oscillator quality.

V. Embodiments and Parameters

Carriers: ISM/licensed; Δf≈1–10 MHz (typ. bandwidth/10); T≈10–20 µs for baseline operation with extensions beyond 100 µs in atomic mode; sub‑sampling rate ≈2×Δf. Calibration modes: none/loopback/perfect. Mobility and packet loss are tolerated via iterative updates and Kalman pre‑filters. Cyclostationary signatures may be imposed by periodically gating or coding the comb so that receivers exploit known second‑order statistics when reconstructing the beat in missing‑fundamental deployments or in low SNR conditions.

Cyclostationary Excitation Signatures

Periodic burst patterns or coded symbol sequences embed a predetermined cyclostationary signature atop the comb. Receivers apply cyclic autocorrelation or spectral correlation techniques to isolate the signature, recovering time of arrival and frequency offset even when the direct Δf tone is filtered or submerged in noise. The gating duty cycle is chosen to satisfy regulatory masks while maximizing cyclic feature energy, and the same signature doubles as a diagnostic handle for field deployments.
